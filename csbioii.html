Frequently Asked Questions

I've heard about microwave in SPPS? Does the CSBio II have it?
Microwave has been successfully implemented in peptide synthesizers, and is used to heat up the synthesis. While some may hypothesize that microwave has other special elements that help with SPPS, it has been shown that microwave is only another form of heating. Heating is the driver that increases the synthesis reaction, speeding up the SPPS process, as well as in some situations improving crude purity.

The primary downside of microwave is that it is a technology that increases the cost of the system, without added benefit over other methods of heating. Additionally, microwave can cause hot spots and easily overheat the reaction, resulting in a poor synthesis.

The CSBio II utilizes conduction heating on both the reaction vessel and the transfer vessels. This allows the solvent to come into the reaction vessel also heated. Conduction heating allows for consistent and uniform heating throughout the synthesis, while also allowing for a most cost effective equipment.

See this case study that was presented by a university for further information about this topic.

How much solvent does the CSBio II use?
The use of solvent within a peptide synthesizer is dependent on the synthesis protocols utilized. There is generally a trade-off between decreasing solvent utilization and peptide purity; as reducing the amino acid excess, deprotection solvent, or washing solvents will generally impact the crude purity of the peptide.

Every peptide sequence has a different level of synthesis difficulty based on the sequence itself and the length of the peptide, where an increasing level of synthesis difficulty will result in a worse crude purity (making it very difficult to purity the peptide) or not even being able to identify the target peptide in the MS. At CSBio, we advertise the solvent utilization based on a synthesis protocol that will work for nearly all peptides. This will result in slightly higher consumption of solvents, however it will avoid having to adjust protocols and re-perform the synthesis to achieve the target peptide.

For more information on this topic, see these two case studies: Demystifying Purity, Synthesis Speed, and Waste Generation and Comparison of Microwave and Conduction Heating
